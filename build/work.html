Web design

Featured work

Focus on Force. I did the web design and development for the Orlando Sentinel's 10-month investigation into use of force by the Orlando Police Department. This required collaborating with reporters, editors, videographers and data analysts, as well as obsessive device and browser testing. To improve load time, I built in JavaScript functionality to load assets (like photos and graphics) only when users are about to scroll to them. The project went on to take first place in the multimedia category of the 2016 Florida Society of News Editors contest.

In Crisis. I also did the web design and development for the Orlando Sentinel's weeklong series on mental illness in Central Florida. The project went on to take first place in the multimedia category of the 2015 Florida Society of News Editors contest.

Soul of Orlando. It was my job to take a reporter's extensive look into what defines Orlando and make it usable and pretty online. The interactive components were also my responsibility. I used JavaScript libraries like React to make this project pop and techniques like lazy loading and preloading to deal with image-heavy pages.

Featured work

Florida breweries map. This map uses the magic of React to bind Leaflet map points to a JSON list of breweries. For development, I used a robust Webpack setup that included ES2015 compiling via Babel, CSS modules and hot-module replacement.

Orlando police uses of force. This one is another combination of React and Leaflet. It adds Leaflet MarkerCluster to reduce the chaos. I wrote the JavaScript in the format of Node.js modules, then bundled everything with Browserify. Thanks to my GIS-whiz colleague, who built the GeoJSON file.

Central Florida homicides. The region might be known for Disney, but it's not without its share of violent crime. Reporters do all the data entry for this project, so it made sense to power it with Tabletop. From there, it's Leaflet for the map and Handlebars for the information.

Precinct-by-precinct Florida primary results. Who doesn't like to know how their neighbors voted in an election? OK, the map isn't that specific — but it does give granular insight into how Florida voted in the 2016 presidential primary. The map is powered by React and Leaflet and relies on ajax to only pull in a single county's geography at a time. My co-worker did lots of data magic to make this work, too.

Active Orlando Police Department calls. A scraper gathers active-call data from a city website and dumps it on a map.

Florida redistricting maps. A colleague handled the geography files, and I handled the code. The result? An interactive look at how the state's congressional districts changed as a result of redistricting.

Elections

Less than six months after starting at the Orlando Sentinel, I was assigned a 10,000-pound gorilla of a project. The task: Build an election scraper that can provide general- and municipal-election results for two news organizations: Orlando Sentinel and the South Florida Sun-Sentinel.

Fast forward nearly two years and two iterations of the project later, and I have delivered three successful elections as the only developer working on the tool. What technology have I used? Lots of Python and Django jazz, including work with Django Bakery and Beautiful Soup, as well as scripting with PHP.

I also built this results page for the 2016 presidential primary, this one for the 2016 non-presidential primary and this one for the 2014 general election.

Knight Foundation grant

In early 2015, the Knight Foundation offered $35,000 Prototype Fund grants to certain organizations that could answer the following question: "How might we better inform voters and increase civic participation before, during and after elections?"

A colleague and I spearheaded the application process for the Orlando Sentinel. We proposed a project called "Tabs on Tallahassee," which would consist of an open-source web application and API that unlock voting records tucked away on the Florida House and Senate websites.

The foundation liked our idea and awarded us the money. For the next six months, my coworker and I served as project managers, ensuring key milestones were met and that our contracted developers had what they needed. We now have a fully functioning web application, and we recently had a hackathon around the API.
